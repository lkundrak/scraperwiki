What is ScraperWiki?

There’s lots of useful data on the internet – crime statistics, government spending, missing kittens…

But getting at it isn’t always easy. There's a table here, a report there, a few web pages, PDFs, spreadsheets… And it can be scattered over thousands of different places on the web, making it hard to see the whole picture and the story behind it. It’s like trying to build something from Lego when someone has hidden the bricks all over town and you have to find them before you can start building!

To get at data, programmers write bits of code called ‘screen scrapers’, which extract the useful bits so they can be reused in other apps, or rummaged through by journalists and researchers. But these bits of code tend to break, get thrown away or forgotten once they have been used, and so the data is lost again. Which is bad.

ScraperWiki is an online tool to make that process simpler and more collaborative. Anyone can write a screen scraper using the online editor. In the free version, the code and data are shared with the world. Because it's a wiki, other programmers can contribute to and improve the code.

If you're not a programmer, or if you want to tackle a large or complex task, you can ask the ScraperWiki team to organise it for you.

Company history and people

ScraperWiki was started by Julian Todd and Aidan McGuire. The company has investment from EV and the founders. We're based in Liverpool, with staff also in London and New York.

Francis Irving (CEO, @frabcus) was founding developer at democracy startup mySociety. He founded developer tool TortoiseCVS, which with its successors is used by tens of millions of people. Aine McGuire (CMO, @ainemcguire) previously founded Pygmalion, the first UK Microsoft Gold Certified Partner company, which has a major focus on education and partner skills. Julian Todd (Chief Data Scientist, @goatchurch) created TheyWorkForYou.com, being first to scrape the UK Parliament back in 2003, inspiring the Sunlight Foundation and others. He dives and caves, but would never cave dive. Aidan McGuire (CFO, @amcguire62) founded three software businesses under the Blue Fountain brand — Japanese spreadsheets, supply chain management and content management. He has a banking background.
David Jones (First Engineer, @drjtwit) has coded garbage collectors, programming language implementations, video games, robot controllers, and analyses of the global historical temperature record. He is a climber, and director of Climate Code Foundation. Chris Blower (Platform Engineer, @chrisb33) got his start programming on Acorn computers, and has since coded everything from telnet talkers to web-enabled telephony applications. He enjoys learning different technologies, flashpacking and photography. Zarino Zappia (VP of Product, @zarino) studied Open Data development at the Oxford Internet Institute, but is really a design geek at heart. When not knee-deep in jQuery and Photoshop, he makes amazing cookies (of the baked, rather than web browsing, kind). Thomas Levine (Developer Advocate, @thomaslevine) has been hacking with computers since he was young. In the process, he noticed back and wrist pain, prompting him to research physical ergonomics of computer use and, in turn, to study statistics. His favorite color is pink. Kinga Deneka (Sales Support Manager, @kinga82) has a background in public administration and spent some of her training at The Chancellery of the President of the Republic of Poland.

Thanks also to: Richard Pope, product manager for the first version of ScraperWiki. Tom Mortimer-Jones, Ross Jones, Anna Powell-Smith, Mike Richardson, and Sym Roe who helped to build it. Nicola Hughes for everything. Judith Townend for co-ordinating our Hacks and Hackers tour in 2010/11. Mark James for Silk icons and Yusuke Kamiyamane for Fugue icons. Tom Steinberg and Jamie Arnold for mentoring. And Francis Davey for legal advice. We also offer bespoke igloos.

ScraperWiki is an Open Source and proud. Head over to BitBucket and inspect our code, file a bug, or submit a patch: http://bitbucket.org/ScraperWiki/

Since you were probably wondering, our logo is a ‘wheel tractor-scraper.’ It’s like a huge cheese slice for moving soil. The logo itself was designed by James Weiner.
